Embedded progromming vs just programming "these days ..."
After hanging around in the field for a bit, I think I know the differences between
the embedded development and development "these days."

Development these days target a platform, instead of a hardware;
meaning, Android programming targets Android, the platform, thus, it is
considered as an Android programming.
Another example is a web development, which targets the browser.

On the other side of things, embedded development targets a hardware, instead
of the software platform; for example, developing for BeagleBone is generally
is considered as an embeded development, due to the fact I am targetting a
hardware to program.

We have a weird relationship happening in Android programming because AOSP
in itself is already embedded (because it's targetting ARM Cortex-M chips),
so, when we program the AOSP itself, it becomes the embedded Android programming,
but programming on top of AOSP becomes just android development ...

I reached out to Mines Alum, and this is what he told me ... 1. Re programming in academia.
Researchers usually don't care about optimizing or scalability.
They care about SPEED. If your dirty/bruteforce solution works and yields some results, that's great.
You want to be the FIRST one to get your research work published before anyone else does
(e.g., We found a solution to this problem, here's our result so far...we will iterate more in future publications).
In industry, people focus a lot more on implementation/engineering.
Most companies have code review in the process so your code will be evaluated by other engineers before going to production
(i.e., you don't want your new feature to crash the entire website which can lose billions of dollars).
If you write bad code every time, you likely will have a bad performance review and won't get promoted.
And no, you won't have to disregard any wisdom and intuition you have gained thus far from the industry.
In fact, I find that people who have interned/worked in the industry actually have better software engineering standards and habits.
In academia, try to strive a balance between speed and quality.
In most cases, don't "overengineer/perfect" your solution.
2. Depending on your advisor.
Always show up on time and ready to lab meeting or one-on-on discussion with your them.
This means have an agenda of talking points. Also, have your own opinions.
Professors tend to like students who can defend their own ideas and support with work with prior literature/research work.
Don't just agree with them because they are your professors. Professors can be wrong, too.
Be independent. Read relevant research papers (maybe 3-4 per week)
to be up to date with your research field and be informed of how other
researchers are investigating similar problem so you don't repeat their work.
Researchers always aim to do novel work.
Lastly, try to schedule at least one check-in meeting with them every week so they know what you're up to.
You are given lots of independence so if you don't check with them frequently,
you may go in a different direction than what your professor may expect. Communication is key.
